MYC Gene Summary

The MYC gene provides instructions for making a protein called c-Myc. This protein is a transcription factor, meaning it binds to specific regions of DNA and helps control the activity of genes. The c-Myc protein plays a critical role in cell growth, division (proliferation), and programmed cell death (apoptosis). It is also involved in the production of ribosomes, which are cellular structures that make proteins.

The MYC gene is a proto-oncogene, which means that it has the potential to become an oncogene, a gene that can cause cancer, if it is mutated or abnormally expressed. Normally, MYC is tightly regulated, but when dysregulated, it can drive uncontrolled cell growth and division, leading to tumor formation.

MYC Gene Function

The c-Myc protein influences several important cellular processes:

  • Cell Cycle Progression: Promotes the transition from the G1 to S phase of the cell cycle, leading to cell division.
  • Apoptosis: Can induce apoptosis, particularly in the absence of growth factors or survival signals.
  • Metabolism: Regulates genes involved in glucose metabolism, glutamine metabolism, and other metabolic pathways.
  • Ribosome Biogenesis: Increases the production of ribosomes, enhancing the cell's ability to synthesize proteins.

Because of its broad effects, MYC dysregulation can have profound consequences for cell behavior and contribute to the development of cancer.

Conditions Related to MYC Gene Mutations

Changes in the MYC gene are associated with a variety of cancers, including:

  • Burkitt Lymphoma: Often caused by a translocation that places the MYC gene under the control of a highly active promoter, leading to overexpression.
  • Breast Cancer: MYC amplification and overexpression are frequently observed in breast cancer, contributing to cell proliferation and tumor growth.
  • Lung Cancer: MYC dysregulation is implicated in the development and progression of lung cancer.
  • Other Cancers: Changes in MYC are also seen in colon cancer, ovarian cancer, and other types of cancer.

These changes can include gene amplification (increased copies of the gene), translocations (rearrangements of the gene's location), and increased expression of the MYC protein.
